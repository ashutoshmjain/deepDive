Main Episode with drum rolls:

The story of enterprise computing isn't a straight line; it's a pendulum swinging between centralization and decentralization. In the 1960s, we had the mainframe, the "Cathedral" of computing, housed in a "Glass House" data center. These were massive, expensive machines, and access to them was controlled by a "priesthood" of operators.

Then came the minicomputer and the workstation, the "Bazaar" of computing, which brought power to the people and ushered in an era of decentralization. But now, with the rise of AI, we're seeing a return to the Cathedral.

Our new paper, "The Cathedral and the Cloud," argues that the modern AI data center is the new Glass House. The NVIDIA H100 GPU cluster is the new mainframe. It's too expensive for individuals to own, and it's controlled by a new priesthood of AI researchers and MLOps engineers. We're back to a centralized, capital-intensive model, where the machine is the master.

And the parallels don't stop there. The "automation anxiety" of the 1960s is back as AGI fears. The "Nifty Fifty" stock market bubble of the 1970s, which was fueled by the tech giants of the day like IBM and DEC, looks a lot like the "Magnificent Seven" of today.

We even see parallels in the hardware itself. The specialized Lisp machines of the 1980s, which were designed for AI, were ultimately crushed by the volume economics of general-purpose chips. Is the same fate awaiting the specialized AI chips of today?
