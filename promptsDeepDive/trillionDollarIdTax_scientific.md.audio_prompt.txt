Main Episode with drum rolls:

The internet was never designed for what it has become. It was built as a decentralized library of documents, not a global marketplace and social hub. And in that original design, there was a critical omission: a way to know who you are. No built-in identity layer. This "Original Sin" of the internet led to the creation of the user account, a solution that has now become a multi-trillion-dollar problem.

Our latest paper, "The Sovereign Key," quantifies this problem. We've all got dozens, if not hundreds of accounts. The average person, according to a 2024 study, has 255 of them. And each of those accounts takes time to manage. Logging in, resetting passwords, creating new ones. We calculated that this "Password Tax" is costing the global economy almost 2 trillion dollars a year. That's the entire GDP of Canada, just vanishing into thin air because of a 30-year-old architectural flaw.

But what if there was a better way? What if, instead of hundreds of accounts, you had one single, sovereign identity that you controlled? This is the promise of the Nostr protocol. Nostr, which stands for "Notes and Other Stuff Transmitted by Relays," is a simple but powerful protocol that gives you a "Sovereign Key" - a cryptographic identity that you own and control.

It works like this: you have a private key that you keep secret, and a public key that you can share with anyone. Every action you take online is signed with your private key, and anyone can verify it with your public key. No more usernames and passwords. No more centralized servers holding your data hostage. Your identity is yours, and yours alone.

And the killer app for this is something called NIP-46, or Nostr Connect. It's a way to use your Nostr identity to log into any website, without ever giving up your private key. It's a universal login that's more secure and more private than anything we have today.
